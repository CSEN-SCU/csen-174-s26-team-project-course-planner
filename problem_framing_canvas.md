# Problem Framing Canvas: SCU undergraduate course planning

**Date:** Thursday, April 9, 2026

---

## Phase 1: Look inward

### What is the problem? (Symptoms)

Students—especially as they gain choice—often end up with **suboptimal course selections** (e.g., workload and fit that don’t match what they actually wanted for that term or that type of class).

### Why haven’t we solved it?

- **Hard:** Large course space, prerequisite and offering complexity, and hard-to-navigate systems (e.g., **Workday**).
- **Lack of resources:** Few accessible aids for comparing options and tradeoffs at planning time.

### How are we part of the problem? (Assumptions and biases)

- **Ratings as the main decision signal** — treating aggregated ratings as the best proxy for “good” choices without validating how students actually decide.
- **Implicit “easier is better”** — partly true for some requirements, but wrong for courses in areas students care about; **priorities need to vary by course type / context** (e.g., core vs. major-interest).

**Which of these might be redesigned, reframed, or removed?**

Treat **teaching quality** and **difficulty / workload** as separable dimensions where data allows, and let students express **priorities by course type** so recommendations don’t collapse to a single “optimize ratings” rule.

## Phase 2: Look outward

### Who experiences the problem?

**Who:** **Upperclassmen** (more electives and scheduling freedom); **transfer students** (less familiarity with offerings and faculty); **students working significant hours** (little time for planning); **international students** (similar friction around navigation, load, and unfamiliarity).

**When / where:** **Quarter planning**, especially around enrollment and schedule building in institutional tools (e.g., **Workday**).

**Consequences:** **Lower confidence in GPA / workload outcomes**, **stress**, and **extra advising cycles** (time cost).

**How lived experience varies:** Pain is higher when students must compare many options with sparse, scattered guidance; it is lower when paths are **fixed or mostly chosen for them**.

### Who else has this problem?

**Who else:** Students at **other schools** with similar catalog complexity and fragmented planning tools.

**How they deal with it:** Informal advice, spreadsheets, word of mouth, rate-style sites, and advisor appointments—often **slow or uneven**.

### Who doesn’t have it (as much)?

**Who doesn’t:** Students **studying abroad** (constrained choices), students in their **last quarter or semester** (narrower decisions), **first-years** with **pre-enrolled** schedules (less planning burden).

**What’s different:** Less open-ended choice, or schedules already largely determined.

### Who’s been left out?

Explicitly brought into scope for framing: **transfers, working students, international students**—not only “typical” upperclass planners. **Accessibility:** intent to meet **ADA** expectations (e.g., **screen reader**–compatible design) so the tool is not only for one mode of use.

### Who benefits?

**When the problem exists (messy, slow planning):** Status quo can **shift burden to advisors** and **peer networks**; students with time and insider knowledge navigate better than those without.

**When the problem is reduced (clearer, faster planning):** Students gain **time and confidence**; **who may feel pressure to adapt** includes roles that today absorb clarification work (e.g., **advising** if volume or type of questions shifts)—worth monitoring as the product evolves, even if the product’s intent is to **automate what students already try to do themselves**.

## Phase 3: Reframe

### Stated another way, the problem is

SCU undergraduates—especially **upperclassmen** during **quarter planning**, and **transfer, working, and international** students with less familiarity or time—struggle to **choose a course mix that matches their real priorities** (e.g., lighter load for some requirements vs. rigor where they care about the subject) because **planning information is scattered, Workday is hard to navigate, and “good choice” is easy to misread if ratings are over-weighted or treated as one-dimensional**, which leads to **lower GPA confidence, stress, and repeated advising cycles**.

### How might we…

**How might we** help students turn **academic progress**, **priorities (difficulty vs. quality)**, and **course type** (e.g., core requirement vs. major-interest) into **clearer, explainable next-term choices** **as we aim to** reduce **stress, advising back-and-forth, and low confidence** during quarter planning?
